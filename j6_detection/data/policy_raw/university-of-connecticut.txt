Web cookies
 (also called 
HTTP cookies
, 
browser cookies
, or simply 
cookies
) are small pieces of data that websites store on your device (computer, phone, etc.) through your web browser. They are used to remember information about you and your interactions with the site.
Purpose of Cookies:
Session Management
:
Keeping you logged in
Remembering items in a shopping cart
Saving language or theme preferences
Personalization
:
Tailoring content or ads based on your previous activity
Tracking & Analytics
:
Monitoring browsing behavior for analytics or marketing purposes
Types of Cookies:
Session Cookies
:
Temporary; deleted when you close your browser
Used for things like keeping you logged in during a single session
Persistent Cookies
:
Stored on your device until they expire or are manually deleted
Used for remembering login credentials, settings, etc.
First-Party Cookies
:
Set by the website you're visiting directly
Third-Party Cookies
:
Set by other domains (usually advertisers) embedded in the website
Commonly used for tracking across multiple sites
Authentication cookies
 are a special type of web cookie used to 
identify and verify
 a user after they log in to a website or web application.
What They Do:
Once you log in to a site, the server creates an authentication cookie and sends it to your browser. This cookie:
Proves to the website that you're logged in
Prevents you from having to log in again on every page you visit
Can persist across sessions if you select "Remember me"
What's Inside an Authentication Cookie?
Typically, it contains:
A 
unique session ID
 (not your actual password)
Optional metadata (e.g., expiration time, security flags)
Analytics cookies
 are cookies used to 
collect data about how visitors interact with a website
. Their primary purpose is to help website owners understand and improve user experience by analyzing things like:
How users navigate the site
Which pages are most/least visited
How long users stay on each page
What device, browser, or location the user is from
What They Track:
Some examples of data analytics cookies may collect:
Page views and time spent on pages
Click paths (how users move from page to page)
Bounce rate (users who leave without interacting)
User demographics (location, language, device)
Referring websites (how users arrived at the site)

What is generative AI
Generative AI is a technology for conversational information searches that “can create original content—such as text, images, video, audio or software code—in response to a user’s prompt or request” (IBM, 2024) and which “learns to generate more objects that look like the data it was trained on.” (Zewe, 2023) In everyday usage, GenAI typically refers to chatbot interfaces trained on large bodies of data and algorithmic models, which can create natural language responses and images. Examples include Copilot, ChatGPT, Gemini, Llama, Claude, Midjourney, Dall-E, and others. “GPT” stands for General Pre-trained Transformer, a reference to an algorithm developed in the late 2010s using neural network type processing to increase the accuracy and speed of generated texts and images.

Do many students use it?
Both students and instructors increasingly use AI as part of teaching or learning. A study from 2024 showed that 30 percent of students reported using generative AI and more than fifty percent reported positive attitudes toward AI. There is confusion, however, about which professors allow AI to be used in projects, and where the boundary lies between appropriate collaboration and cheating. For these reasons, students may either avoid using AI entirely or use it inappropriately.

What should my policy on AI be?
While the university does not have a blanket AI policy, AI use is governed by the 
Data Classification Policy
, the 
Academic, Scholarly, and Professional Integrity and Misconduct Policy
, and the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act of 1974 (
FERPA
). Instructors are encouraged to put an AI policy on their syllabi. AI policies typically range from banning the use of AI to fully integrating it into every aspect of the course. Most courses adopt policy language somewhere in the middle.
It is important to remember that generative AI tools range from simple autofill in emails, through paraphrasing tools like Grammarly, to highly sophisticated generative chatbots like ChatGPT. We recommend your syllabus statement include information on at least the following details:
Which tools are okay to use?
How do you plan to hold students accountable for AI use?
Why do you have the policy you have?

How can I build student AI literacy and fluencies in my class?
Including AI or addressing it with your students will help prepare them for jobs or further work in most disciplines, in addition to building important technological literacy more broadly. AI use varies by discipline, profession, or enterprise. Please reach out to CETL for 1:1 consultations on integrating AI into activities and assignments in alignment with your course objectives.
The foundations of AI literacy are:
Knowing when to use AI and when not to
Knowing how to ask and refine questions with an AI chatbot (“prompting”)
Critically evaluating AI output
Conceptual understanding of generative AI models and algorithms
Identification of a wide range of risks in AI and AI enterprises

Is there any evidence that AI helps students learn?
Early 2023 marked the arrival of ChatGPT 3.5 and with it, a turning point in generative AI. Since then, research on generative AI and learning has exploded and continues to grow. Studies generally fall into two broad categories: 1) what students need to know about AI, and 2) unique affordances of AI for learning. This section focuses on the latter.
To date, studies of the unique affordances of AI for learning focus on learning outcomes such as:
Outcomes related to personalization in support of learning
Outcomes for creativity
Outcomes for critical thinking
Outcomes related to self-efficacy & motivation
Outcomes for second language learning
It is challenging to operationalize a multi-dimensional concept like learning. For this reason, there is currently no reliable consensus on AI’s effects on learning. Whether that is unique to AI or applies across teaching methods and technologies is an open question contingent on many contextual factors. For example, in a comparison of digital technologies, Cubillos et al (2025) found that students using generative AI reported enhanced feelings of autonomy and choice, which can benefit learning, while students using videos to learn reported greater sense of competence. One meta-analysis (Sun & Zhou, 2024) showed a greater effect size for independent learning with AI than for collaborative learning, across disciplines; however, it is not clear whether this captures student preferences rather than an effect of instructional design. These are just two examples of the complexity of studying AI’s impact on learning.

What are some AI tools I can use in my teaching?
UConn has a contract with Microsoft for basic access to Copilot. It is free to faculty, staff, and students.  Enhanced subscriptions can also be purchased by departments. In both cases, the contract affords privacy protections and includes an agreement that queries will not be used to train large language models. Due to these protections, it is recommended, though not required, that you use Copilot.
This industry changes constantly, with new tools being added, enhanced, or removed.
The other most prominent chatbots include ChatGPT (owned by OpenAI), Llama (owned by Meta), Claude (owned by Anthropic), and Google Gemini (owned by Alphabet).  Each has its own algorithms and configuration of data sets on which the model is trained. For image generation, Midjourney and DALL-E (and SORA for video) currently dominate. With a paid ChatGPT account, you can access custom GPTs (models) for, e.g., data analysis, diagnostics, etc., or create your own “GPT” through detailed instructions. Copilot does not feature custom GPT creation, but there are ways to instruct it that come close, especially in Copilot Plus (paid). If you are interested in doing this, you may find this 
Custom GPT Guide
 helpful.
When deciding on a tool and working with it, follow these practices:
Understand FERPA and follow UConn’s Data Classification Policy
Connect the choice to your course learning objectives
Transparently communicate to students your definition of plagiarism and cheating
Communicate to students the risks of AI
Require students to demonstrate comprehension of what is “identifiable information” so they can avoid entering it into an AI tool

Should I use detection software?
Currently detection software is of limited use and may be detrimental because it generates false negatives and, more importantly, false positives. Still, this technology continues to develop and is an important part of the AI landscape. For example, methods for protecting artists’ work and copyright involve detecting or in some cases distorting or blocking a file to prevent it from training image generation models like DALL E and Midjourney. Similarly, scholars are attentive to the need for transparency in what publishing companies do with their articles and books, e.g., selling them to AI companies. While this is more a copyright than detection issue, it points to a node of issues related to fair labor and academic integrity. The United States Copyright Office published 
guidance on copyright
 in January 2025.

Another model is the “
AI marble layer cake
” approach (Cardamone), which borrows from the flipped classroom philosophy. Essentially this approach incorporates AI into both homework and in-class work, but the emphasis in class is on analyzing and discussing the AI-generated output students got when they did the homework.
The marble layer cake metaphor joins the “
AI sandwich
” idea (Ippolito). The very simple idea behind this approach is that students use AI as a brainstorming partner first, then spend time with other students in class, engaging in “human-to-human knowledge creation,” and finally use AI again as a writing partner.

What does generative AI do to assessment of learning?
While safeguarding the integrity of assessment practices is critical, it is equally important to recognize that cheating and plagiarism often stem from reasons other than lack of ethics. For example, students may not know what acceptable collaboration looks like; acceptable collaboration may differ from instructor to instructor, or tool to tool; students may be overwhelmed by the course material or other priorities in their lives such as jobs or family duties. There is a lot we can do, pedagogically, to scaffold assignments, make content accessible, and communicate the value and purpose of using generative AI critically and judiciously.
UConn’s University Senate, and the Office of the Provost, maintain guidelines for academic integrity and scholastic standards. The 
UConn Office of Community Standards
 offers information on handling plagiarism and cheating.

AI-Resilient Learning Outcomes
 (MIT Sloan School of Management) The AI-Resilient assignments toolkit walks you through taking an inventory of your students’ prior experience with AI, program of study, and other contexts; then mapping AI activities onto your course learning objectives; and finally scaffolding related activities. This process highlights what is needed to secure the reliability of assessment even with the use of AI.

Zachari Swiecki, Hassan Khosravi, Guanliang Chen, Roberto Martinez-Maldonado, Jason M. Lodge, Sandra Milligan, Neil Selwyn, Dragan Gašević, Assessment in the age of artificial intelligence,
Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence, Volume 3, 2022, https://doi.org/10.1016/j.caeai.2022.100075.

The goal of this article is to describe the opportunities and threats of AI-assisted assessment. The authors critique traditional assessment practices and examine whether AI can address some of these drawbacks. Drawbacks include: the burden of designing assessments; the one-size-fits-all application to student knowledge, skills, and backgrounds; the question of what assessments actually assess and whether they are assessing what 
should
 be assessed; and the problem that assessments might “be tailored to the culture of schooling rather than the cultures schooling is designed to prepare students to enter.” For a range of assessments from exams to creative projects, the article provides concrete examples of both AI’s affordances and limitations (and risks).

How are UConn instructors using AI in teaching?
Getting practice and gaining familiarity with AI tools is the best way to learn about their benefits in different contexts and for different tasks. AI tools will even tell you what the risks of AI are, if you ask it to. Well-known risks include loss of critical thinking, privacy violations, copyright violations, harm to labor in the global South, environmental impact on water and energy us, and even addiction to using AI chatbots. Goepfrich, and Graduate Student, Lillian Schaefer-Calderon, for an engaging and informative session on the evolving role of Generative AI, especially Large Language Model tools, in academic settings. This presentation will cover key topics to help faculty better understand and navigate these emerging technologies in the classroom while upholding academic integrity. 
Key topics include: 
Understanding Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) and Large Language Models (LLM): Learn the basics of these tools, their potential uses, and the challenges they pose in academic environments. 
Fostering Positive Use: Explore strategies for incorporating Generative AI tools constructively to enhance learning while maintaining academic integrity. 
Setting Clear Expectations: Discover best practices for establishing course guidelines regarding the use of AI tools to promote transparency and fairness. 
Addressing Misuse: Understand how to handle Generative AI or Large Language Models misuse in violation of Academic, Scholarly, and Professional Integrity and Misconduct Policy. 
Student Perspective: Gain insight into how students view and use these technologies, presented from a graduate student’s viewpoint. 
University Statistics: Review relevant university data on the impact and prevalence of AI tool usage in cases referred for academic misconduct.

October 23, 12:30–1:30 Why immersion in GenAI is essential for future creatives engaged in creative work
In this workshop, Matt Worwood, Digital Media & Design Assistant Professor in Residence, will briefly introduce generative AI from the perspective of creativity before engaging in a journey to explore prevalent themes within the domain of generative artificial intelligence and its potential influence over the creative process of a future professional called Aria Novak. 
The presentation will highlight some of the topics explored within the field of creativity and propose some of the skills and talents most likely to remain at the forefront of skills professionals in the future. A proposed discussion topic at the end of the workshop is how we might promote or develop these skills within today's educational settings.

October 30, 12:00–1:00 AI as Research Ally: An Introduction to Generative Tools for Students and Faculty Research
Explore the potential of AI as an academic research partner in this introductory session. We'll look at tools such as Perplexity, Research Rabbit, Scite, Elicit, and Consensus, and how these tools might complement research efforts. Designed for those new to AI, this session will provide a supportive environment to get curious about how these tools could assist with literature reviews, citation management, discovering connections, and more. We'll conclude with a Q&A, so bring your questions and ideas for future AI research tool workshops!
